Ultrasonic Spray Coating for Fuel Cell Applications

Proton exchange membrane (PEM) fuel cells require precise, uniform catalyst layers on the membrane or gas diffusion layer (GDL) to achieve optimal electrochemical performance. Ultrasonic spray coating has become the preferred method for MEA fabrication due to its unmatched combination of coating uniformity, catalyst utilization efficiency, and process scalability.

The Challenge in Fuel Cell MEA Fabrication

The catalyst layer in a PEM fuel cell MEA consists of platinum (Pt) or platinum-alloy nanoparticles supported on carbon black, dispersed in a Nafion ionomer solution. The coating must be:

  • Highly uniform in loading (target: ±2% across the active area)
  • Thin enough to minimize mass transport resistance (typically 5–20 μm)
  • Porous to allow gas diffusion while maintaining ionic conductivity
  • Deposited with minimal waste of expensive platinum catalyst

Conventional air-spray methods struggle to achieve the required uniformity and result in 30–50% catalyst waste due to overspray. Ultrasonic spray coating addresses all these challenges simultaneously.

How Ultrasonic Spray Coating Works for MEA

The SonoTech ultrasonic spray coating system atomizes the catalyst ink (Pt/C + Nafion ionomer in IPA/water solvent) using a 48–120 kHz ultrasonic nozzle, producing droplets of 20–50 μm. The fine mist is deposited onto the membrane or GDL substrate mounted on a heated platen (60–80°C) that evaporates the solvent immediately upon contact, preventing membrane swelling and ensuring a porous, well-adhered catalyst layer.

The XYZ motion system moves the nozzle in a programmed raster pattern over the substrate, building up the catalyst loading in multiple thin passes for maximum uniformity. Loading is controlled by adjusting flow rate, scan speed, and number of passes.
