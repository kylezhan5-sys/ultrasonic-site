Complete Guide to Ultrasonic Spray Coating

Published: 2024-03-15

What is Ultrasonic Spray Coating?

Ultrasonic spray coating is a precision thin-film deposition technique that uses high-frequency mechanical vibration (typically 25-120 kHz) to atomize liquids into a fine, uniform mist. Unlike conventional air-spray methods that rely on pressurized air to break up liquid, ultrasonic atomization uses the vibrational energy of a piezoelectric transducer to create standing waves on the liquid surface, which break up into droplets when the vibration amplitude exceeds a critical threshold.

Key Advantages Over Conventional Spray Methods

The fundamental differences in atomization mechanism give ultrasonic spray coating several significant advantages:

  • Narrow droplet size distribution: CV typically below 15%, vs. 40-60% for air spray
  • High material utilization: 90-95% vs. 30-50% for air spray
  • No pressurized air required: Eliminates contamination and turbulence
  • Gentle atomization: Preserves sensitive materials including proteins, nanoparticles, and polymers
  • Precise flow control: Independent control of droplet size and flow rate

Key Process Parameters

Nozzle Frequency: The primary determinant of droplet size. Higher frequency = smaller droplets. Common frequencies and typical droplet sizes:

FrequencyTypical D50Best For
25 kHz60-100 umThick coatings, high flow rates
48 kHz35-55 umGeneral purpose, catalyst inks
60 kHz25-40 umThin films, fine coatings
120 kHz10-20 umUltra-thin films, biological materials

Flow Rate: Controlled by the liquid delivery system (syringe pump or peristaltic pump), independent of droplet size. Lower flow rates produce thinner coatings per pass; multiple passes can build up to the target loading.

Scan Speed and Pattern: The XY motion system speed and pattern determine the coating thickness per pass and the uniformity across the substrate. Bi-directional raster scanning with 50% overlap between passes is the standard approach for uniform coatings.

Substrate Temperature: Heating the substrate (typically 60-120°C) accelerates solvent evaporation, preventing pooling and improving coating adhesion. The optimal temperature depends on the solvent system and substrate material.

Nozzle Selection Guide

Selecting the right nozzle frequency depends on your target droplet size, which in turn depends on your application requirements:

  • For fuel cell and electrolyzer catalyst coatings: 48-60 kHz (25-50 um droplets)
  • For perovskite and organic solar cell layers: 60-120 kHz (10-40 um droplets)
  • For medical device coatings: 60-120 kHz (10-40 um droplets)
  • For thick industrial coatings: 25-48 kHz (40-100 um droplets)

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Non-uniform coating: Check nozzle height (typically 5-15 cm from substrate), scan speed, and overlap percentage. Ensure substrate is level and heater is uniform.

Clogging: Filter the liquid to remove particles larger than 50 um. Flush the nozzle with clean solvent between runs. Check liquid viscosity (should be below 200 cP for standard nozzles).

Droplet bouncing: Increase substrate temperature or reduce flow rate. Droplet bouncing occurs when the substrate cannot absorb the liquid fast enough.
