Ultrasonic Spray vs. Air Spray Coating: Technical Comparison

Published: 2024-04-05

Introduction

When selecting a spray coating method for thin-film applications, the choice between ultrasonic spray and conventional air spray has significant implications for coating quality, material efficiency, and total process cost. This article provides a detailed technical comparison to help you make the right choice for your application.

Atomization Mechanism

Air Spray: Pressurized air (typically 0.5-5 bar) shears the liquid stream into droplets at the nozzle tip. The droplet size distribution is wide (CV 40-60%) and depends on air pressure, liquid viscosity, and nozzle geometry. High air velocity creates turbulence that can cause overspray and non-uniform deposition.

Ultrasonic Spray: High-frequency vibration (25-120 kHz) creates standing waves on the liquid surface. When the vibration amplitude exceeds the critical threshold, the liquid surface breaks up into droplets of a size determined by the vibration frequency. The droplet size distribution is narrow (CV below 15%) and independent of flow rate.

Material Utilization

Material utilization is the percentage of the sprayed material that actually deposits on the target substrate. This is a critical economic factor for applications using expensive materials such as platinum catalysts.

  • Ultrasonic spray: 90-95% material utilization. The fine, slow-moving droplets follow the substrate surface closely and deposit efficiently.
  • Air spray: 30-50% material utilization. High-velocity air carries many droplets past the substrate as overspray.

For a platinum catalyst worth $30,000/kg, the difference in material utilization between ultrasonic and air spray can save $15,000-20,000 per kg of catalyst used.

Coating Uniformity

Coating uniformity (typically expressed as the coefficient of variation of coating thickness or loading across the substrate) is critical for applications like fuel cell MEA where non-uniform catalyst loading directly reduces performance.

  • Ultrasonic spray: ±2-5% uniformity achievable across 200x200 mm with optimized scan pattern
  • Air spray: ±10-20% uniformity typical; difficult to improve due to air turbulence

When to Choose Air Spray

Air spray remains the preferred choice when: (1) coating area is very large (above 1 m2) and uniformity requirements are moderate, (2) the liquid is too viscous for ultrasonic atomization, (3) very high flow rates are required, or (4) equipment cost is the primary constraint.

Conclusion

For precision thin-film applications with expensive materials, ultrasonic spray coating offers compelling advantages in material utilization, coating uniformity, and process control that typically justify the higher equipment investment. The ROI is particularly strong for applications using precious metal catalysts.
