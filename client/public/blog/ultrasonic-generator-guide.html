How to Select an Ultrasonic Generator

Published: 2024-01-10

What Does an Ultrasonic Generator Do?

An ultrasonic generator (also called an ultrasonic power supply or driver) converts standard AC mains power (50/60 Hz) into a high-frequency electrical signal that drives a piezoelectric transducer. The generator must match the transducer's resonant frequency precisely to achieve maximum energy transfer efficiency. A well-matched generator-transducer system will operate at 90-95% electrical efficiency.

Key Selection Parameters

1. Frequency

The generator frequency must match the transducer's resonant frequency. Common frequencies are 20 kHz, 25 kHz, 28 kHz, 40 kHz, and higher frequencies for specialized applications. The frequency determines the type of application:

  • 20-25 kHz: High-power welding, cutting, homogenization
  • 28-40 kHz: Medium-power welding, cleaning, processing
  • 60-200 kHz: Precision spray coating, fine cleaning
  • 1-3 MHz: Ultrasonic atomization

2. Power Output

Select power based on your application requirements. Undersizing the generator will result in insufficient amplitude and poor process results. Oversizing wastes energy and cost. As a general guide:

  • Spray coating nozzles: 5-100W
  • Small homogenizers (up to 500 mL): 100-500W
  • Industrial homogenizers: 500-3000W
  • Plastic welding (small parts): 500-1000W
  • Plastic welding (large parts): 2000-5000W

3. Auto-Frequency Tracking (AFT)

Auto-frequency tracking is essential for any serious application. The resonant frequency of a transducer shifts by 50-500 Hz as it heats up during operation. Without AFT, the generator operates off-resonance, reducing efficiency and potentially damaging the transducer. All SonoTech generators include AFT with a tracking range of ±500 Hz.

4. Control Modes

Modern digital generators offer multiple control modes:

  • Constant Amplitude: Maintains fixed vibration amplitude regardless of load. Best for welding and cutting where consistent amplitude is critical.
  • Constant Power: Maintains fixed power output. Useful for homogenization and sonochemistry where energy input is the key parameter.
  • Constant Current: Maintains fixed current to the transducer. Useful for some specialized applications.

Compatibility Considerations

When replacing a generator or selecting one for an existing transducer, you need to match:

  • Frequency: Must match transducer resonant frequency within ±500 Hz (AFT range)
  • Impedance: Generator output impedance should match transducer impedance at resonance
  • Power: Generator power should be 10-20% above the transducer's rated power
  • Connector: Physical connector type must match
